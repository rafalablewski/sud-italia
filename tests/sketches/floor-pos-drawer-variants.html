Five advanced variants of the docked check drawer

The drawer is the right chassis — the check, in place, over the floor. These five push it from a good modal into a best-in-class service surface: it resizes to intent, holds several checks at once, reads the situation and leads, settles a split cleanly, and choreographs the meal by course. Each is a distinct capability; together they describe one adaptive drawer.

One PosTab engine underneath Every variant is additive — they compose Floor stays live behind
Read this as one drawer with five modes, not five drawers. They share the same summon-on-tap, the same CorePos core, and the same dismiss. You're choosing which capabilities to turn on first — nothing here trades against anything else.
1

Progressive drawer — peek · half · full

→ shows exactly as much as the moment needs

The drawer snaps to three widths. Peek is a slim spine: table, total, and Pay. Half adds the line items and quick verbs. Full is the complete till with the menu grid. Drag the edge or tap a snap to escalate; it remembers the last size per role. A server glancing at a total never pays the cost of the whole till; a bartender building a big order gets it all.

2

Multi-check dock — several tables, one rail

→ juggle a section without losing a check

Tapping a second table doesn't replace the check — it stacks it as a tab along the drawer's edge. A server working a five-table section keeps all their live checks in one dock, switching between them like browser tabs, each remembering its scroll and course state. A pulsing dot flags a table that needs attention (food up, bill waiting) even while another is in front.

3

Anticipatory drawer — it reads the check and leads

→ the next right move, one tap away

The drawer opens with a next-best-action band at the top: it reads the check's live state — food's up, unpaid for 14 minutes, a course is held, an allergy is on file, the kitchen's backed up — and promotes the single most urgent verb as a big primary button, colour-coded to urgency. The full till sits below, untouched. The server is guided, never hunting.

4

Split & settle drawer — the end of the meal, solved

→ the pay flow that never sends anyone to a terminal

When a check heads to payment, the drawer becomes a settlement surface: a row of covers, items assigned by tap or drag, one-touch split by cover or by item or evenly, and integrated card · cash · QR with tip — each cover paid independently, the drawer tracking the remaining balance live. Nobody walks to a till to close a table.

5

Course-timeline drawer — the meal as choreography

→ fire, hold and pace by course from the check

The drawer organises the check as a vertical course timeline — Antipasti · Primi · Secondi · Dolci — each course a fire/hold unit synced to the KDS. Sent courses show green; the next is a one-tap fire; held courses wait until the table's ready. A pacing hint reads the kitchen and the table's dwell ("Secondi held 12m — fire now?"), so the server conducts the meal instead of reacting to it.

Synthesis — one adaptive drawer

Build them as layers of a single drawer, in this order. Start with 1 · Progressive — it's nearly free and makes every later layer feel lighter. Add 3 · Anticipatory next: the action band is the highest-value-per-effort move, turning the drawer from a form into a guide. Then 2 · Multi-check for rush-hour throughput. Finally the two service specialists — 5 · Course timeline for how dine-in actually flows, and 4 · Split & settle to own the payment moment end to end.

Because all five share one CorePos core and one PosTab truth, the result isn't five features bolted on — it's a drawer that resizes to intent, holds your whole section, reads the room, conducts the courses, and settles the bill, all without a single trip to a terminal.
